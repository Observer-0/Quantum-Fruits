Abstract

The quest for a unified framework connecting Quantum Mechanics (QM) and General Relativity (GR) has been a central endeavor of theoretical physics for over a century. While QM successfully describes microscopic phenomena through superposition, uncertainty, and quantized action, GR provides an elegant geometric theory of spacetime that explains macroscopic dynamics from planetary orbits to cosmology.

Despite their empirical success, both theories remain fundamentally separated on extreme scales. General Relativity predicts spacetime singularities—inside black holes or at the beginning of the universe—where curvature and energy density diverge and the classical description breaks down. Quantum Field Theory encounters ultraviolet divergences in vacuum energy, leading to the so-called vacuum catastrophe—a discrepancy of about $10^{122}$ between theoretical estimates and the observed cosmological constant.

This work demonstrates that this gap closes naturally when introducing the Planck two-measure

$$\sigma_P = \ell_P t_P = \frac{\hbar G}{c^4}$$

a minimal, invariant spacetime cell completely defined by fundamental constants. This quantity regulates vacuum fluctuations, eliminates classical singularities through Planck-covariant source averaging, and determines the cosmological constant without free parameters.

Core Statement

The only valid fundamental theories of nature are Quantum Field Theory and General Relativity. No new "theory of everything" is needed to connect them. What is required is merely to resolve the known inconsistencies within these two pillars—ultraviolet divergence and classical singularity—through the natural structure already contained in the constants $\hbar$, $G$, and $c$.

The Planck two-measure $\sigma_P$ provides exactly this missing link.

First, the minimal, singularity-free form of Einstein's field equations in a finite Minkowski spacetime with the naturally determined cosmological constant

$$\Lambda = \frac{3}{cRt}$$

is presented—in the spirit of Occam's razor. This minimal form contains no additional structures beyond what Quantum Mechanics, General Relativity, and the Planck scale themselves require.

The energy-momentum sector of the equation can be modularly extended when physically necessary—but never through ad hoc additions. Different kernels or averaging procedures may be used, but the geometric foundation remains universal.

This work does not propose a new cosmological model or a replacement for ΛCDM. It provides the missing geometric invariant that allows the existing foundations of physics to act consistently across all scales.
