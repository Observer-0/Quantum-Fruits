What is the Page Curve?

Named after physicist Don Page, this curve tracks the entanglement entropy of Hawking radiation.

In a classical scenario (Hawking 1974), the entropy rises until the black hole vanishes—meaning information stays trapped forever. Page proposed that if quantum mechanics is correct (Unitarity), the entropy must reach a maximum (at Page Time) and then fall back to zero.

Why does the Curve fall?

The "falling" is the signal of Information Recovery. In the Zander Framework, this is triggered by the c₀ non-thermality.

As the black hole evaporates, the relative importance of the $\sigma_P$ graining increases. The radiation stops being "noise" and becomes a coherent carrier of the interior state. The internal degrees of freedom are "squeezed" into the radiation, reducing the black hole's own entropy.

The Challenge: Information Loss

In 1974, Stephen Hawking showed that black holes emit radiation. However, this radiation appeared to be purely thermal, meaning it contains no information about the matter that fell in.

If the black hole evaporates completely, the information is lost forever. This violates the fundamental principle of Unitarity in Quantum Mechanics, which states that information must always be preserved.

The Σₚ Resolution

The Zander model introduces the Axiom of Finite Divisibility. Spacetime is not infinitely smooth; it has a minimum action grain ($\sigma_P$).

  • No Singularity: The temperature cannot grow to infinity.
  • Unitary Return: Information is encoded in the discrete correlations of the spacetime graining.
  • The Page Curve: As the simulation shows, entropy eventually drops as information is "squeezed" back into the observable universe.
